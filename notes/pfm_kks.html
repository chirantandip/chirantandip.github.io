The KKS Model

This Chapter presents a summary of the Kim-Kim-Suzuki Model. (The Notes are still in making.)

The Binary Solidification Model

This phase field model was first proposed in the paper1 as a binary model for solidification of multicomponent systems. Since then it has been developed and applied to study and model a range of microstructure evolution phenomenon from eutectic solidification to precipitate growth.

The model is free from the limit in the interface thickness in the WBM model, and the unrealistic assumptions made by Losert, and it correctly generates the solute trapping phenomena at high interface velocity. A few relevant points about the model are:

  • It is an isothermal model.

  • Defines n+1 components, and phases, solid and liquid.

  • ciSc_{iS}, ciLc_{iL} represent mole fraction of ii’th solute in solid and liquid, they are scalar fields.

  • The Helmholtz free energy of a phase fp=f({cip})f^p = f(\{c_{ip}\}), is a function of solute concentrations only.

  • The interface is defined as a mixture of solid and liquid phases.

  • The free energy at interface follows the mixture rule : finterface=h(ϕ)fS+[1−h(ϕ)]fLf^{interface}=h(\phi)f^S + [1-h(\phi)]f^L

  • The concentration at interface also follows the mixture rule : cinterface=h(ϕ)cS+[1−h(ϕ)]cLc^{interface}=h(\phi)c^S + [1-h(\phi)]c^L

  • And ciSc_{iS} and ciLc_{iL} in a given point are restricted by the equal chemical potential condition.

  • The material properties are assumed to be independent of the composition.

The total free energy of the system:

F=∫V(ϵ22|∇ϕ|2+wg(ϕ)+h(ϕ)fS+[1−h(ϕ)]fL)dVF=\int_V \left( \frac{\epsilon^2}{2}|\nabla \phi|^2 + wg(\phi) + h(\phi)f^S + [1-h(\phi)]f^L \right)dV

where ϵ\epsilon : gradient energy coefficient, g(ϕ)=ϕ2(1−ϕ)2g(\phi)=\phi^2(1-\phi)^2 : double well potential and ww : potential height.

Imposing the equal chemical potential condition upon the solid and liquid phases at a point of the system has two advantages over the traditional equal composition condition.

  • The first is the relaxation of the restriction on the interface width in computation.

  • The second is that the profile of the equilibrium phase-field gradient becomes symmetric, which suppresses the anomalous nonlinear part in the Gibbs–Thomson effect in the thin interface limit.

The phase-field and diffusion equations from the work of Kim1 and Eiken2 are presented here:

1Mϕϕt=ϵ2∇2ϕ−Wdg(ϕ)dϕ−dhp(ϕ)dϕ(fS−fL+∑i=1n(cS−cL)μĩ)\frac{1}{M_\phi} \phi_t = \epsilon^2\nabla^2\phi - W \frac{dg(\phi)}{d\phi} - \frac{dh_p(\phi)}{d\phi}\left( f^S - f^L + \sum_{i=1}^n(c_S - c_L)\tilde{\mu_i} \right)

where ϵ\epsilon is the gradient energy coefficient, MϕM_\phi is the mobility constant and WW is the double well potential barrier height.

The diffusion equation is as such:

∂ci∂t=∇⋅(hd(ϕ)∑j=1nDijS∇cjS+[1−hd(ϕ)]∑j=1nDijL∇cjL)\frac{\partial c_i}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left( h_d(\phi) \sum_{j=1}^n D^S_{ij} \nabla c_{jS} + [1-h_d(\phi)] \sum_{j=1}^n D^L_{ij} \nabla c_{jL} \right)

A point to be noted is that, the concentrations of solid (cSc_S) and liquid (cLc_L) are defined at a certain infinitesimal point which is assumed to be a mixture of solid and liquid phases. Therefore the equality of chemical potentials imposed is also local. The chemical potential need not stay constant throughout the interfacial region. It is only constant across the interface only at a thermodynamic equilibrium state.

At the interfacial region the components also satisfy the equality of chemical potential and the solute concentration is taken to be

c=h(ϕ)cS+(1−h(ϕ))cLfcSS[cS(x,t)]=fcLL[cL(x,t)]c = h(\phi)c_{S} + (1-h(\phi))c_{L} \; \; \; \; \; \; f^S_{c_S}[c_S(x,t)]=f^L_{c_L}[c_L(x,t)] \label{eq:c-constraints}

Assuming g(ϕ)=ϕ2(1−ϕ)2g(\phi)=\phi^2(1-\phi)^2, ϕ0=1\phi_0=1 (solid) at x=−infx=-\inf and ϕ0=0\phi_0=0 (liquid) at x=+infx=+\inf, the equilibrium profile solved for 1D is given as:

ϕ0=12(1−tanhw2ϵx)\phi_0=\frac{1}{2}\left( 1 - \tanh\frac{\sqrt{w}}{\sqrt{2\epsilon}} x \right)

From ϕ0\phi_0 we get the the interface energy σ\sigma and interface thickness 2λ2\lambda to be σ=ϵw322λ=α2ϵw\sigma=\frac{\epsilon\sqrt{w}}{3\sqrt{2}} \; \; \; \; \; \; 2\lambda=\alpha\frac{\sqrt{2}\epsilon}{\sqrt{w}}

where α\alpha is a constant depending on the definition of interface thickness. α=2.2\alpha=2.2 when ϕ0∈(0.1,0.9)\phi_0\in(0.1,0.9).

Introducing the Anti-trapping current

When we define a finite interface width, several anomalous interface effects appear. This was shown my Almgren3. The effects can be negated by using interpolation functions with specific symmetry but it appears that all of them cannot be suppressed simultaneously.

For dilute binary alloys with DS<<DLD_S<<D_L, Karma solved this problem by introducing an anti-trapping term in the diffusion equation45. Kim6 extend the anti-trapping method to the cases of the arbitrary multi-component alloys. The assumption DS<<DLD_S<<D_L is, however, was maintained because it allowed the concentration (or chemical potential) profile at steady state to be determined unambiguously.

The anti-trapping term is introduced to the diffusion equation as:

∂ci∂t=∇.[1−hd(ϕ)]∑j=1nDijL∇cjL+∇⋅αi∂ϕ∂t∇ϕ|∇ϕ|\frac{\partial c_i}{\partial t} = \nabla .[1-h_d(\phi)]\sum_{j=1}^n D_{ij}^L\nabla c_{jL} + \nabla \cdot \alpha_i\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t}\frac{\nabla \phi}{|\nabla \phi|} \label{eq:compevol}

where αi\alpha_i is a function of ciSc_{iS} and ciLc_{iL}, and cic_i is given by ci=hr(ϕ)ciS+(1−hr(ϕ))ciLc_i = h_r(\phi)c_{iS} + (1-h_r(\phi))c_{iL}

Interpolation functions.

The three equations, phase evolution, diffusion equation and mass conservation, have three different interpolation functions marked by p,d,rp,d,r. Even though a single function h(ϕ)h(\phi) must be adopted in the rigorous thermodynamic derivation, it is not the case in mapping the diffuse interface model onto the classical sharp interface model45.

However, a specific symmetry in their functional forms must be imposed in order to suppress the anomalous interface effects, such as interface diffusion and interface stretching. This symmetry condition is equivalent to the requirement that the positions of the effective sharp interfaces for the driving force action (hph_p), diffusivity change (hdh_d) and solute partitioning (hrh_r) must be in accordance with that of the effective Gibbs–Thomson interface which is the symmetry axis position of the potential g(ϕ)g(\phi). For the potential g(ϕ)=ϕ2(1−ϕ)2g(\phi)=\phi^2(1-\phi)^2 having a symmetry axis at x=1/2x=1/2, the simple interpolation functions such as ϕ\phi satisfy the symmetry condition. Even after the choice of functions, there remains an anomalous interface effect: the chemical potential jump at the effective sharp interface.

Chemical potential jump.

Kim6 assumed that the interface width is sufficiently smaller than the diffusion boundary layer width in liquid, that is, the thin interface condition. Note that ciSc_{iS}, ciLc_{iL} and μĩ\tilde{\mu_i} are constrained by equality of chemical potential. If one of them is known at a given point, the other two at that point are fixed by the condition. For an interface with a finite width, there exist a finite difference between ciS+c_{iS}^+ and ciL−c_{iL}^-. This makes a corresponding difference in chemical potential, which has been called the chemical potential jump4.5

For multicomponent systems with arbitrary thermodynamic properties, as for dilute binary alloys45 the chemical potential jump can be suppressed by a suitable choice of the interpolation functions and α(ciS\alpha(c_{iS}, ciL)c_{iL}), that is, by balancing the anomalous solute trapping arising from the diffusion through the thick interface.

The procedure for balancing the solute trapping with the anti-trapping current is straightforward.

  1. Find the composition profile ciL(x)c_{iL}(x) by solving the steady-state diffusion equation.

  2. Extract the straight part from the profile ciL(x)c_{iL}(x) and then get ciS+c_{iS}^+ and ciL−c_{iL}^-.

  3. Put ciS+=ciL−c_{iS}^+ = c_{iL}^- to determine the interpolation functions and α(ciS\alpha(c_{iS}, ciL)c_{iL}) for the condition of vanishing chemical potential jump.

The interpolation functions are found to be hr(ϕ)=hd(ϕ)=ϕh_r(\phi)=h_d(\phi)=\phi and for a vanishing chemical potential jump the anti-trapping function is given as

αi=2ωϵ(ciL−ciS)\alpha_i = \frac{\sqrt{2\omega}}{\epsilon}(c_{iL}-c_{iS})

The parameters ϵ\epsilon and ω\omega can be found from their relationships with the interface width 2ξ2\xi and the interface energy σ\sigma in the equilibrium state:

2ξ=ϵ2ϖ∫ϕaϕbdϕ0ϕ0(1−ϕ0)=ϵ2ϖlnϕb(1−ϕa)ϕa(1−ϕb)2\xi=\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{2\varpi}}\int_{\phi_{a}}^{\phi_{b}}\frac{d\phi_{0}}{\phi_{0}(1-\phi_{0})}=\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{2\varpi}}ln\frac{\phi_{b}(1-\phi_{a})}{\phi_{a}(1-\phi_{b})}

σ=ϵ2∫−∞∞(dϕ0dx)2dx=ϵϖ32\sigma=\epsilon^{2}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(\frac{d\phi_{0}}{dx})^{2}dx=\frac{\epsilon\sqrt{\varpi}}{3\sqrt{2}}

where 2ξ2\xi is defined as the width over which ϕ\phi changes from ϕa\phi_a to ϕb\phi_b.

Phase-Field Mobility

The phase-field mobility MϕM_\phi has a relationship with the interface mobility mm defined by the ratio between the driving force and the interface velocity. The procedure to find MϕM_\phi is as such:

  1. Find the profile ciL(x)c_{iL}(x) under the condition ciS+=ciL−c_{iS}^+ = c_{iL}^- vanishing chemical potential jump.

  2. Then ciS(x)c_{iS}(x) and μĩ\tilde{\mu_i} follow from equality of chemical potential constraint.

  3. Insert the composition and chemical potential profiles into the driving force term of the phase-field equation and extract the new driving force for the effective sharp interface with the straight composition profiles in the interfacial region.

  4. The relationship between the new driving force and the interface velocity is found, which yields a relationship between the physical interface mobility mm and the phase-field mobility MϕM_\phi at the thin interface limit.

Ultimately it leads to:

fL,e−fS,e−∑i=1n(ciLe−ciSe)μ̃i0=V(1Mϕw32ϵ−a2ϵ2σζ)f^{L,e} - f^{S,e} - \sum_{i=1}^n (c^e_{iL} - c^e_{iS}) \tilde\mu_i^0 = V \left( \frac{1}{M_\phi} \frac{\sqrt w}{3 \sqrt2 \epsilon} - a_2 \frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{2 \sigma}} \zeta \right)

where ζ\zeta is defined as: ζ=∑i=1n(ciLe−ciSe)∑j=1nfijL,e∑k=1ndjkL(ckLe−ckSe)\zeta=\sum^n_{i=1}(c^e_{iL}-c^e_{iS})\sum^n_{j=1}f^{L,e}_{ij}\sum^n_{k=1}d^L_{jk}(c^e_{kL}-c^e_{kS})

This can be written into a compact form

ζ=(Δc][GL][DL]−1[Δc)=(Δc][ML]−1[Δc)\zeta = (\Delta c ] [G^L] [D^L]^{-1} [\Delta c) = (\Delta c] [M^L]^{-1} [\Delta c) with the matrix notations: (Δc]=ciLe−ciSe(\Delta c] = c^e_{iL} -c^e_{iS}, [GL]=fijL,e[G^L] = f^{L,e}_{ij} and [ML]=MijL[M^L] = M^L_{ij}.

In particular, for the infinite interface mobility, we obtain the phase-field mobility

Multi-Component extension

The KKS model discussed above is expanded to a multi-component system with the following modifications.

The Phase-Evolution-Equation (Allen-Cahn Style) can be derived from the variational derivative of the total free energy as such:

∂ϕp∂t=−LN∑q≠pN[δFδϕp−δFδϕq]\frac{\partial \phi_p }{\partial t} = -\frac{L}{N} \sum_{q\neq p}^{N}{\left[ \frac{\delta F}{\delta \phi_p} - \frac{\delta F}{\delta \phi_q}\right]}

The Diffusion-Equation becomes :

∂ci∂t=∇⋅∑j=1k−1(Mij(ϕ)∇μij)\frac{\partial c_i }{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \left( M_{ij}(\phi) \nabla \mu_{ij} \right) where Mij(ϕ)=∑p=1N[hp(ϕ)∑j=1[Dikpdckdμj]] : Dij=Mik∂f∂ck∂cjM_{ij}(\phi) = \sum_{p=1}^{N} \left[ h_p(\phi)\sum_{j=1}\left[D^p_{ik}\frac{d c_k}{d \mu_j}\right] \right] \text{ : } D_{ij} = M_{ik}\frac{\partial f}{\partial c_k \partial c_j}

The constraint imposed on the concentration fields are mass conservation and equality of chemical potential implemented as such:

cj=∑p=1Nh(ϕp)cjp;μj=μi:∂fp∂cip=∂fp∂cjp c_j = \sum_{p=1}^{N} h(\phi_p)c^p_j ; \mu_j = \mu_i : \frac{\partial f^p}{\partial c^p_i} = \frac{\partial f^p}{\partial c^p_j}

The multi-well potential is chosen to be:

g(ϕ)=∑i=1Nqγiϕi2(1−ϕi)2+∑i=1N∑j>iNθijϕi2ϕj2+∑i=1N∑j>iN∑k>jNθijkϕi2ϕj2ϕk2g(\phi) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} q \gamma_i \phi_i^2(1-\phi_i)^2 + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j>i}^{N} \theta_{ij}\phi_i^2\phi_j^2 + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j>i}^{N} \sum_{k>j}^{N} \theta_{ijk} \phi_i^2\phi_j^2\phi_k^2 or one can use the multi-obstacle potential.

The Phase-Field interpolation function is

h(ϕp)=ϕ3(1−15ϕ+6ϕ2)h(\phi_p) = \phi^3 (1-15\phi + 6\phi^2)
